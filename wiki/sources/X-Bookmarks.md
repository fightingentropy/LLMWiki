### On profit-taking (markets)

> The adage "no one ever went broke taking profits" is a (potentially) dangerous fallacy because it centers the exit decision on your entry price. It treats profit-taking as a psychological reward rather than a strategic forecast. For any strategy that doesn't win the majority of the time, outsized winners are the essential engine that funds your inevitable losses. By "locking in" a gain simply because your P&L is green, you are systematically underfunding the very outliers required for your survival. An exit must be forward-looking, based on what the price is likely to do next rather than where you happened to buy. The market has no memory of your cost basis, and anchoring your decisions to it is a form of self-sabotage.
> — **@0xaporia** (Aporia), #391 (from [[X-Bookmarks]])

> for my less mathematically inclined friends — you are a pump fun trencher that loses 50% on 80% of the coins you buy, and makes 5x on the other 20% (EV +40%) — if you aggressively TP at 2x, you literally will go broke taking profits if you continue playing this game (EV -20%)
> — **@Cheshire_Cap**, #392 (from [[X-Bookmarks]])

### On selling the top

> In my experience, I've found that it is often more dangerous to sell the top than to sell on the way down. This is because the euphoria associated with selling the top can lead to early rebidding as well as a sense of overconfidence.
> — **@zhusu** (Zhu Su), #890 (from [[X-Bookmarks]])

### On Discovery Bounds / 24/7 perps

> To realize the radical concept of 24/7 trading, we made an explicit trade-off: limit the extreme tails of price movement, and the range within becomes substantially more conducive to genuine price discovery. ... Discovery Bounds V2 ... maintains active boundaries which prevent rapid swings in mark price, preserving the vital protective properties of the first iteration. But the bounds are no longer static — when trading persists at the edge of the active range, the oracle gradually converges toward that level.
> — **@0xmev** (shadow), #128 — relevant to [[Exchange Microstructure]], [[Hyperliquid]] (from [[X-Bookmarks]])

> If you haven't accomplished much by 25, you may have escaped one of youth's biggest illusions. ... In your early 20s, the brain seeks quick proof of worth — status, attention, rapid achievements. People who feel "behind" in their mid-20s frequently gain something others miss: Perspective. Patience. And a clearer sense of what truly matters to them. ...
