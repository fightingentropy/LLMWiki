# BS EN 61557

**BS EN 61557 — "Electrical safety in low voltage distribution systems up to 1000 V AC and 1500 V DC — Equipment for testing, measuring or monitoring of protective measures"**. The performance / accuracy standard for the test instruments used to verify that a fixed electrical installation complies with [[BS 7671]].

Where [[BS EN 61010]] guarantees the instrument is **safe**, 61557 guarantees it is **accurate enough** for the verification readings to actually mean something.

## What the Standard Covers

From the source notes: *"BS EN 61557 (IEC 61557) — Covers: accuracy specifications, measurement reliability, test result validity, performance standards. Requirement: test equipment should provide accurate measurements per this standard."*

The standard is published in numbered parts, each covering one type of installation test. The key parts for a UK electrician:

- **61557-1** — General requirements common to all parts
- **61557-2** — **Insulation resistance** measurement (used at 250 V / 500 V / 1000 V DC)
- **61557-3** — **Loop impedance** (line-earth, line-line) — covers Ze, Zs, Zdb readings
- **61557-4** — **Resistance of earth connection and equipotential bonding** — the low-resistance ohmmeter / continuity test
- **61557-5** — **Resistance to earth** (earth electrode resistance, typically for TT systems)
- **61557-6** — **Residual current devices (RCDs)** — tripping time and tripping current tests
- **61557-7** — **Phase sequence** indicators
- **61557-8** — **Insulation monitoring devices** for IT systems
- **61557-9** — **Insulation fault location** in IT systems
- **61557-10** — **Combined (multi-function) test equipment** — the typical MFT found in the field
- **61557-12** — **Performance measuring and monitoring devices** (PMD)
- **61557-13** — **Clamp and hand-held current sensors** for leakage current measurement

## Why It Matters in Practice

[[BS 7671]] requires specific numerical outcomes: a measured Zs below the tabulated maximum, insulation resistance above a stated minimum, continuity within a calculated value. None of those decisions are defensible if the instrument's accuracy spec is unknown.

61557 sets out:

- The **operating range** over which the instrument must meet its accuracy spec (e.g. for insulation resistance, from MΩ up into GΩ)
- The permitted **overall operating uncertainty** at the extremes of that range
- The **influence quantities** (temperature, battery voltage, supply variations) the instrument must tolerate without drifting out of spec
- The **functional safety** of the test sequence itself (e.g. an insulation resistance tester must indicate a live circuit rather than apply 500 V blindly)

This is why instruments stamped "IEC 61557" are the only ones suitable for completing the schedule of test results on an [[EIC and MEIWC|EIC]] or [[EICR]].

## Calibration vs Accuracy Checks

61557 defines how accurate the instrument must be **when in spec**. It does not prove the instrument is **still** in spec on any given day. Two practices cover that gap:

- **Annual calibration** by a UKAS-accredited lab — certificate of accuracy on the calibration date.
- **Regular on-site accuracy checks** against a known reference (calibration box, short link at 0 Ω, a known-value resistor, mains voltage).

From the source notes: *"Calibration is a point-in-time verification... Best practice: perform accuracy checks between calibrations."* The PAT Testing source recommends recording these checks (e.g. Form V6).

## Relationship to Other Standards

- [[BS EN 61010]] — the instrument's **safety** construction and CAT rating.
- [[GS38]] — the **leads and probes** attached to the instrument.
- 61557 — what the instrument **measures and how well**.

All three must be satisfied for compliant installation verification.
