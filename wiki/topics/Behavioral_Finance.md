# Behavioral Finance

The study of how cognitive and emotional biases distort financial decisions and aggregate into observable market patterns. Distinct from classical finance's rational-actor assumption — assumes the actor is predictably irrational.

## In this wiki

Behavioral finance is referenced as a tag on [[Trading_Psychology]] and sits underneath most of the trader-level observations the wiki collects from [[CT]]. Not a standalone thesis — treated as the substrate under which named patterns (bag-holding, variance-driven strategy hopping, reflexive pumps) operate.

## Key themes

- **Capital-allocation asymmetry.** "You act too quickly trying to grow capital and too slowly trying to protect it" — panic selling plus hesitant buying explains most losses (from [[Bizz_and_Finance]], see [[Capital_Allocation_Timing]]).
- **Decision vs. outcome decoupling.** Randomness masks poor reasoning; luck masks bad strategy. Long-term separates signal from noise ([[Trading_Psychology]]).
- **Variance blindness.** Even a 40% win-rate strategy has ~49% probability of an 8+ losing streak over 100 trades — most traders misread noise as strategy failure ([[0xaporia]] in [[CT]]).
- **Narrative post-hoc.** "Any time price moves 10% we literally hallucinate a story up for it" (@CL207) — narrative follows price, not vice versa.
- **First-cycle optimism flipping to cynicism.** Same openness that makes money in early cycles loses it at the end ([[CryptoCred]]).
- **Greed-peaks-late / dilution-kills-bulls.** Greed maxes out in the back half of bull runs; cycle ends when dilution exceeds inflows ([[Pentosh1]]).
