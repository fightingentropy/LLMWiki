## Overview
Natural gas is highly seasonal with significant weather sensitivity and structural policy dynamics affecting long-term demand.

## Key Drivers

### Supply-Side
- **Major gas field production**: Russia (before sanctions), Middle East, US
- **Production decisions**: Large producers managing output for price management
- **LNG exports**: Liquefied natural gas export capacity and demand
- **New project development**: Arctic, deepwater, offshore

### Demand-Side
- **Weather patterns**: Winter heating demand spikes with cold
- **Industrial demand**: Fertilizer, chemicals, power generation
- **Storage levels**: Strategic and commercial inventory levels
- **Renewable competition**: Wind/solar reducing nat gas power generation

### Policy & Geopolitics
- **Energy policy**: Support for natural gas vs. renewables vs. coal
- **Geopolitical disruption**: Russian sanctions cut European supply
- **LNG sanctions**: Export restrictions or supply disruptions
- **Climate policy**: Long-term pressure from decarbonization

## Seasonality
- **Winter (Oct-Mar)**: Peak heating demand; prices normally higher
- **Summer (Apr-Sep)**: Low demand; prices normally lower
- **Shoulder seasons**: Storage building (summer) and drawing (winter)

## Trading Implications
- **Highly seasonal**: Don't short summer gas, don't go long summer peaks
- **Weather sensitive**: Early cold winter = supply shock, early warm = demand shock
- **Storage indicator**: Monitor storage levels; low storage in winter = risk
- **Geopolitical sensitive**: Supply disruptions can spike prices 50%+
- **Policy sensitive**: Renewable buildout is structural headwind long-term
