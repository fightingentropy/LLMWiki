# Media Critique

The wiki's view of mainstream media: not a marketplace of ideas but a capture mechanism operated by elites to shape the window of acceptable thought, with narrative control as the primary product.

## In this wiki

Anchored in [[Political_Commentary]] under "Media Capture by Elites." Supplemented by observations in [[Thoughts]] on information density and narrative compression.

## Key themes

- **Elite capture.** Wealthy elites have slowly politicized news over decades by owning multiple competing channels (left/right leaning) to trap viewers in a controlled spectrum. "All channels funnel toward the same conclusions; audience thinks they chose truth" ([[Political_Commentary]]).
- **Discrediting by association.** Governments create or promote crazy conspiracy theories to make real ones seem crazy by association. Mix true and false to poison both ([[Political_Commentary]]).
- **Iran uprising as coverage failure.** Western liberal media cannot cover Iranians rebelling against Islam itself because it shatters their moral framework. Racialized Islam as ethnicity rather than ideology. Erases Iranians, strips them of ability to reject the ideology ([[Political_Commentary]]).
- **Moral category shielding.** Admitting Iran's failed state-controlled economy would undermine Western institutions advocating soft versions of the same policies. Selective coverage protects the ideology.
- **Entertainment replacing news.** Education and news replaced with entertainment as part of the attention-and-narrative capture loop ([[Political_Commentary]]).
- **Narrative as interface.** "Storytelling is the ultimate compression algorithm for human attention. If you can't wrap an idea in compelling narrative, nobody cares" — explains why facts don't change minds; narrative matters more than truth ([[Thoughts]], [[Philosophy_and_Meaning]]).
- **Podcasts = low-density medium.** Lowest information density of any medium ever created, but totally passive — poor vehicle for actual learning ([[Thoughts]], [[Technology_Critique]]).
