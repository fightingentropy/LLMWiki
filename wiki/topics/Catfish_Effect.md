# Catfish Effect (鲶鱼效应)

Chinese HR and industrial-policy concept: introduce a deliberate competitive stressor into a stagnant system to prevent complacency. Not destruction — managed agitation, on the thesis that stagnation is the bigger risk.

## In this wiki

Referenced from [[Bizz_and_Finance]] and [[Culture]]. Already has a developed writeup inside [[Business_Strategy]] (where the Tesla-in-China case study lives). This page is the dedicated topic stub that those cross-links were pointing at.

## Key themes

- **Origin story.** Norwegian sardine fishermen transported sardines in tanks; many died from inactivity. Adding a catfish kept the sardines active, more survived. Metaphor travels from fishing to HR to industrial policy ([[Business_Strategy]]).
- **Managed agitation, not disruption.** "Stress on purpose because stagnation is the bigger risk" ([[Bizz_and_Finance]]). Differs fundamentally from Western natural-market disruption — competition is curated, introduced, welcomed.
- **Tesla in China as case study.** Chinese government gave Tesla rare approvals (full ownership, fast-tracked factories, land, logistics) specifically to pressure domestic EV companies who had been cashing subsidies without delivering. Result: pace of innovation accelerated across the industry ([[Business_Strategy]]).
- **Requires trust and long-term thinking.** Absent in quarterly capitalism. Implicit argument that Western systems lack the coordination to deploy this tool ([[Business_Strategy]]).
- **Parallels acute vs. chronic stress.** The biological analogue of the Catfish Effect is acute stress — deliberately introduced stressor that activates the system. See [[Stress_Management]], [[Body_Optimization]].
- **HR-level application.** Original business-school version: introduce a high-performer into a complacent team to raise the floor. Same mechanism, smaller scale.
