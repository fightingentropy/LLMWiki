# Part P

Part P is the electrical safety section of the Approved Documents to the UK Building Regulations. It sets out where and when electrical installation work in dwellings must be notified to Building Control and certified to BS 7671.

## Scope

Part P applies to fixed electrical installations in:

- **Dwellings** — where people permanently reside (from [[Building Regulations]] notes and `raw/electrics/Resources.md`: "Part P: applies where people permanently reside (dwellings).")
- **Common parts of buildings** that serve one or more dwellings (shared areas in multi-unit buildings)
- **Associated outbuildings** such as garages, sheds, and garden lighting fed from the dwelling supply

It does **not** cover non-domestic installations (commercial, industrial, agricultural). Those are regulated under other Building Regulations Parts plus [[BS 7671]] and the Electricity at Work Regulations.

## Status

- Part P is **guidance** under the Approved Documents, not statutory law in itself. The Building Regulations are the law; Approved Document P explains one acceptable way to comply.
- Compliance route is almost always via [[BS 7671]] — the wiring regulations.
- Work must be designed, installed, inspected, and tested so the installation is safe to use and maintain.

## Notifiable Work

Certain domestic electrical work is **notifiable** to Building Control:

- Installation of a new circuit
- Replacement of a consumer unit (distribution board)
- Any addition or alteration in a "special location" (per the edition in force — historically bathrooms/shower rooms and areas around swimming pools, with scope narrowed over time)

Non-notifiable work (e.g. adding a socket to an existing circuit, replacing an accessory) still must comply with [[BS 7671]] and be certified via [[EIC and MEIWC]] — but doesn't need Building Control involvement.

## Routes to Compliance for Notifiable Work

Three routes exist for getting notifiable work signed off:

1. **Competent Person Scheme (CPS)** — the installer self-certifies and notifies the local authority via the scheme. Examples: NICEIC, NAPIT, ELECSA, STROMA. Registration involves assessment of competence and ongoing audits.
2. **Third-party certification** — an unregistered installer uses a registered third-party inspector to certify the work before notification.
3. **Direct Building Control notification** — notify the Local Authority Building Control in advance; they inspect and charge a fee.

Just holding qualifications to sign an [[EIC and MEIWC|EIC]] is **not enough** to self-notify — CPS registration is required for that route.
