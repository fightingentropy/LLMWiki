# EICR

**Electrical Installation Condition Report**. The formal document produced by a periodic inspection and test of an existing fixed electrical installation. Often described via the "MOT analogy" — it reports on the condition of an installation in use, rather than certifying new work.

## Distinction From EIC / MEIWC

- **EIC** — new circuit/new installation certification (see [[EIC and MEIWC]])
- **MEIWC** — alterations/additions to one existing circuit
- **EICR** — condition report on an existing installation; no new work involved

This split is captured in the source notes: *"Initial verification: Certification of new work... Condition reporting/EICR: Reporting on existing installations in use (like MOT analogy)"*.

## Purpose

An EICR answers: is this existing installation safe for continued use?

It records:
- General installation characteristics (supply, earthing, bonding, distribution)
- Visual inspection results against the schedule of conditions
- Dead and live test results
- Observations and their classification codes
- Overall assessment: **Satisfactory** or **Unsatisfactory**
- Recommended date for the next inspection

## Observation Codes

Each defect or deviation found is given a code:

- **C1 — Danger present.** Risk of injury. Immediate remedial action required. Any C1 makes the report Unsatisfactory.
- **C2 — Potentially dangerous.** Urgent remedial action required. Any C2 makes the report Unsatisfactory.
- **C3 — Improvement recommended.** Not dangerous, but worth fixing (e.g. brings the installation closer to current [[BS 7671]]). C3s do not by themselves make a report Unsatisfactory.
- **FI — Further investigation required, without delay.** Something couldn't be fully assessed and needs following up. FI makes the report Unsatisfactory.

A report is **Satisfactory** only when there are no C1, C2, or FI observations.

## Landlord Requirements (Private Rented Sector)

The **Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020** require private landlords to:

- Have the fixed electrical installation inspected and tested by a qualified person **at least every 5 years**, or more frequently if the previous EICR recommends sooner
- Obtain an EICR and supply a copy to:
  - Each new tenant before occupation
  - Existing tenants within 28 days of inspection
  - The local authority within 7 days on request
- Carry out any remedial work for C1, C2, or FI observations within **28 days** (or sooner if specified), and obtain written confirmation the installation is now safe
- Retain the EICR and provide it to the next inspector

Similar (but not identical) regimes apply in Scotland, Wales, and for HMOs.

## Format and Content

A compliant EICR includes, at minimum:

- Client, installation address, and purpose of the report
- Extent and limitations of the inspection (agreed with the client in advance)
- Installation characteristics — supply type, earthing arrangement (TN-C-S, TN-S, TT), number of live conductors, nominal voltage, prospective fault current, external earth fault loop impedance (Ze), maximum demand
- Schedule of inspections (visual) with ticks / cross / N/A and codes
- Schedule of test results per circuit
- Summary of the condition of the installation
- Observations and recommendations with C1/C2/C3/FI codes
- Declaration by the inspector (name, signature, qualifications)
- Date of inspection and recommended date of next inspection

See the model forms in [[GN3 Guidance Note 3]] Chapter 5.

## Who Can Produce an EICR

From the source notes: *"With relevant qualifications and genuine competence/experience, a person can sign: EIC, MEIWC, EICR."*

Competence, not just a certificate, is the real requirement. See [[Electrical Safety]] on the competence framework.
