# Genius and Madness

The observation that intuitive decision-makers who thrive on saturated information flow tend to deteriorate into unreliable judgment as that flow thins — which is why the line between genius and madness runs through the same people over time.

## In this wiki

A recurring theme inside [[Trading_Psychology]] and [[Psychology]], usually invoked to explain why preternaturally good callers become preternaturally bad callers later. Not a theory the wiki develops rigorously — it functions as a warning label on intuitive strategies.

## Key themes

- **Intuition is information-bound.** Intuitive traders thrive when plugged in and processing tons of information constantly. Make preternaturally good calls (from [[Psychology]], [[Trading_Psychology]]).
- **Aging thins the flow.** As intuitive operators age and lose info-flow, confident inferences go awry. The confidence persists; the input doesn't.
- **Examples.** Nobel laureates, Howard Hughes, Elon cited in [[Psychology]] as patterns of late-career drift into madness.
- **Measured thinkers are safer.** More careful, process-driven thinkers are less spectacular but less prone to this trap ([[Trading_Psychology]]).
- **Implication for traders.** Build processes that don't depend on a single operator's intuition aging gracefully. Decouple edge from identity.
