## Overview
Interest rates are the foundation of all asset pricing. Rate movements ripple through all markets.

## Key Rate Points

### Front-End (2Y, 3Y)
- **What it measures**: Near-term Fed policy expectations
- **Drivers**: Fed rate guidance, inflation data, employment
- **Meaning**: Steeper 2Y = higher near-term rates expected; flatter = lower rates expected

### Long-End (10Y, 30Y)
- **What it measures**: Long-term growth and inflation expectations
- **Drivers**: Structural growth, long-run inflation, term premium
- **Meaning**: Higher 10Y = higher growth/inflation expected; lower 10Y = lower expectations

### The Yield Curve (Shape)
- **Steep curve**: 10Y far above 2Y; growth/inflation expected
- **Flat curve**: 10Y ≈ 2Y; transition period, uncertainty
- **Inverted curve**: 10Y < 2Y; recession expected

### Real Yields
- **What it is**: Nominal yield minus expected inflation
- **Why it matters**: Real return to savers and borrowing cost
- **Relationship to macro**: Higher real yields = more attractive relative to risk assets

### Implied Policy Path
- **What it is**: Market's expectation for Fed rate path
- **Drivers**: Data, Fed guidance, market repricing
- **Significance**: Gap between market and Fed guidance reveals repricing risk

## Rate-Asset Relationships

### Why Rates Matter
- **Discount rate effect**: Higher rates = lower present value of future cash flows = lower equity values
- **Opportunity cost**: Higher rates = bonds more attractive vs. equities
- **Growth sensitivity**: Rate path reflects growth expectations
- **Financial system cost**: Higher rates = financing costs, corporate profitability pressure

### Rate Moves & Market Impact
- **Expected rate move**: Built into prices; happening as expected = no shock
- **Surprise higher**: Rates higher than expected = shock down (growth concerns OR inflation out of control)
- **Surprise lower**: Rates lower than expected = shock up (growth collapse) OR relief (inflation solved)

## Curve Dynamics

### Curve Steepening
- Front-end rates fall (Fed cutting) while long-end holds = curve steepens
- Usually occurs early in recession before long-end reprices lower
- Trades: Long 10Y / short 2Y benefits from steepening

### Curve Flattening
- Front-end rates rise (higher near-term policy) while long-end falls = curve flattens
- Usually occurs as inflation falls and recession risk rises
- Trades: Short 10Y / long 2Y benefits from flattening

### Curve Inversion
- 10Y < 2Y; indicates recession expected
- Once inverted, stays inverted until Fed cuts rates
- Often precedes recession by 6-18 months
