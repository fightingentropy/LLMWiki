# Peptide Reconstitution

The general procedure for turning a lyophilized (freeze-dried) injectable peptide into a measured subcutaneous dose. Drawn from the [[Retatrutide]] guide (from [[Peptides]]), but the handling, math, storage, and injection knowledge applies to most research peptides.

## The Solvent: Use BAC Water

Reconstitute with [[Bacteriostatic Water]] (0.9% benzyl alcohol), **not** sterile water, saline, or tap water. BAC water's preservative lets a multi-dose vial last up to **28 days** refrigerated; sterile water has no preservative and forces a 24-hour discard.

## The Dilution Math

$$\text{Concentration (mcg/mL)} = \frac{\text{Peptide amount (mcg)}}{\text{Water added (mL)}}$$

$$\text{Units to inject} = \frac{\text{Desired dose (mcg)}}{\text{Concentration (mcg/mL)}} \times 100$$

"Units" are the marks on a standard **U-100 insulin syringe**, where 100 units = 1 mL. Less water → higher concentration → fewer units per dose (but harder to measure small doses accurately). More water → easier small-dose measurement, but large doses may exceed the 100-unit syringe and need splitting.

**Worked example:** 10,000 mcg (10 mg) vial + 2 mL water = 5,000 mcg/mL. A 4 mg (4,000 mcg) dose = 4,000 ÷ 5,000 × 100 = **80 units**. This **10 mg + 2 mL = 5 mg/mL** setup is the commonly recommended default: 1 mg = 20 u, 2 mg = 40 u, 4 mg = 80 u.

## Reconstitution Procedure

1. **Room temperature** — let the vial and BAC water sit out 15–20 min; cold powder dissolves poorly.
2. **Clean** — wash hands; wipe both rubber stoppers with 70% isopropyl alcohol swabs; let air-dry 10–15 s.
3. **Draw BAC water** — inject an equal volume of air into the BAC vial first (equalizes pressure), invert, draw slowly, tap out bubbles.
4. **Add water to the peptide — critical step.** Angle the needle at the **glass wall**, not the powder, and depress the plunger slowly over 10–20 s so water trickles down the side. **Never blast water onto the powder** — it can denature the peptide.
5. **Mix gently — DO NOT SHAKE.** Roll between palms or swirl slowly. Dissolves in 1–3 min to clear/colorless. Shaking foams the solution and shears peptide bonds (can cost 30–50% potency).
6. **Inspect** — should be completely clear and colorless (slight yellow tint acceptable). Cloudy or particulate → discard.
7. **Label & refrigerate** — write compound, concentration, reconstitution date, and 28-day use-by; refrigerate immediately.

## Storage Rules

- **Lyophilized powder:** stable for months (room temp) to years (fridge / freezer). Keep unused vials frozen; only reconstitute what you'll use in ~3–4 weeks.
- **Reconstituted solution:** refrigerate at 2–8 °C, use within **28 days** (BAC water) or 24 hours (sterile water).
- **Never freeze reconstituted solution** — ice crystals shear the peptide bonds.
- **Never leave reconstituted solution at room temperature** — ~10–20% potency loss per day.

## Subcutaneous Injection

- **Route:** subcutaneous (SubQ) only; many of these peptides are **once weekly**, same day each week.
- **Sites:** abdomen (most common), front of thigh, back of upper arm — **rotate** each time, at least 1 inch from the last spot.
- **Technique:** 45°–90° needle angle depending on body fat / needle length; pinch a skin fold; push the plunger slowly over 5–10 s.
- Let the vial warm to room temp 15–30 min before drawing — cold solution stings more.

## Titration Principle

For dose-escalating peptides, ramp the dose gradually rather than starting high — jumping to a high dose causes severe GI side effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea). If side effects are significant at a step, hold there an extra 2–4 weeks before escalating. (See the [[Retatrutide]] schedule for a concrete example: 1 → 2 → 4 → 6 → 9 → 12 mg over ~21+ weeks.)

## Common Mistakes That Ruin a Peptide

- Shaking the vial → denatures, –30–50% potency
- Spraying water onto the powder → damages structure
- Sterile water instead of [[Bacteriostatic Water]] → 24-hour window
- Reconstituted solution left at room temp → potency loss
- Freezing reconstituted solution → destroyed bonds
- Reusing needles → contamination + dulling
- Too little water → can't measure small doses
