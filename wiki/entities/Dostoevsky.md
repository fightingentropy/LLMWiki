# Fyodor Dostoevsky

Russian novelist (1821–1881). One of the most-quoted authors in [[Quotes]] — the human returns to him specifically for lines about self-betrayal, the cost of lying to oneself, and the pathology of overthinking.

## Recurring lines (from [[Quotes]])

- **On cowardice:** "Man has it all in his hands and it slips away out of sheer cowardice." (echoed as "Man has it all in his hands, and it all slips through his fingers from sheer cowardice.")
- **On lying to oneself:** "A man who lies to himself, and believes his own lies becomes unable to recognise truth, either in himself or in anyone else, and he ends up losing respect for himself and for others. When he has no respect for anyone, he can no longer love..."
- **On overthinking:** "I swear to you that to think too much is a disease, a real, actual disease." (appears twice in source)
- **On self-betrayal:** "Your worst sin is that you betrayed yourself for nothing."
- **On human naivety** (*Brothers Karamazov*): "As a general rule, people, even the wicked, are much more naive and simple-hearted than we suppose. And we ourselves are, too."
- **Last words to his wife Anya:** "Remember, Anya, I have always loved you passionately and have never been unfaithful to you ever, even in my thoughts." (appears twice — load-bearing for the human)

## Why he's load-bearing in the wiki

- **Self-betrayal as worst sin** — a recurring theme across [[Quotes]], called out explicitly in the themes section at the bottom of that page. Ties into [[Self_Improvement]]'s "regret comes from unexpressed potential, not failure" and [[Mental_Health]]'s shame-as-parasite framing.
- **Overthinking-as-disease** — direct match for [[Self_Improvement]]'s "aim, fire, correct later" and the enthusiasm-gap observation that dumber-but-faster people lap overthinkers.
- **Cowardice as the default mode** — mirrors the [[Quotes]] "anxiety is what happens when your brains are bigger than your balls" cluster and Napoleon's "courage is the only virtue that escapes hypocrisy."
